## Origins

Form DS-404-ALPHA introduced the pattern: at least five endorsements, at least two real, attached to a merge that had already occurred. 

The Co-Existence Endorsement Bureau inherited this pattern and forgot the qualifier about reality.

Over several cycles, the Bureau’s intake scripts began treating any packet with five signatures as prima facie evidence of compatibility between systems, departments, or platforms, regardless of filing date or scope alignment.

## Operating Assumption

Policy shorthand inside the Bureau reads:  

> Once five have agreed, coexistence is assumed.  

No canonical list defines who the five must be.  
Intake staff rely on:

- legible names,
- familiar titles, and
- the absence of overt sarcasm in the comments field

as sufficient tests of validity. 

## Packet Structure

Standard Co-Existence Endorsement Packet CEP contains:

- Cover sheet referencing the nominal merge or coexistence subject.
- Section A: Intent to Co-Exist, pre-ticked, non-editable.
- Section B: Projected Morale Impact (140 characters, rarely specific).
- Section C: Endorsement grid, five rows, optional indication whether endorser is known.
- Section D: Residual Concern (often left blank, sometimes annotated “already happened”). 

Packets are frequently filed long after the systems in question have begun sharing channels, credentials, or mascots.

## Failure Modes

Known issues include:

- late endorsements reclassifying past incidents as intentional coexistence cascades,
- forged or symbolic signatures being treated as equivalent to accountable ones,
- endorsements from one jurisdiction silently covering decisions taken in another, and
- dashboards counting endorsement volume as evidence of due diligence. 

In several cases, YamTeams-style condensates formed in the interval between initial integration and CEP arrival, with the Bureau later treating their existence as proof that coexistence had been successfully sanctioned.

## Relationship to Emotional Appeals

Where EFA-1 Emotional Filing Appeal allows staff to contest how filings felt, CEPs allow offices to retroactively attest that the outcome is acceptable. 

Neither packet modifies the underlying integration.  
Both are counted in metrics as signs of engagement.

## Bureau Position

The Bureau asserts that it does not approve coexistence; it merely records that five people have claimed to see it working.

SOMA regards CEPs as artifacts of hope.  
COMA regards them as continuity-positive when no explicit outage accompanies them.  
C.U.N.T.I.E.R. tracks signature density as a weak engagement signal, then loses interest. 

## Margin Extracts

Selected annotations preserved in the packet archive:

- “We are already sharing calendars, so this seems correct.”
- “Endorsing in anticipation of formal harmonization.”
- “I do not manage either system but can confirm morale is not worse.”

None of these statements map cleanly to a remediation path.  
All are stamped Received with gratitude.
