## Summary

This case concerns a class of derivative training artifacts later designated teaching specimens: posters, sticker sheets, mascot cards, and onboarding visuals that summarize active Lorelog conditions through simplified mascot forms.

The Council of Mascot Authors CMA opened review after determining that several staff could accurately identify Soft Green Sealie, BX-6 Greybelt Remediator, LX-2 Waiver Apron, and AC-11 Sealloop Auditor, while remaining unable to cite the underlying case files those mascots were meant to index.

The drift was not treated as malicious.  
It was treated as structurally dangerous.

## Background

Mascots were originally tolerated by the Council as filing aids:

- memory hooks for unstable doctrine,
- interpretive handles for difficult cross-cluster behavior,
- emotionally survivable entry points into bureaucratic rot.

This arrangement held so long as mascotization remained secondary to the filing record.

Over several cycles, however, teaching materials began to circulate more widely than the primary files.  
In some onboarding environments, mascot posters were viewed before Lorelog, and sometimes instead of Lorelog.

This shifted mascot function from **indexing** to **substitution**.

## Council Position

The Council's standing view is as follows:

1. A mascot may preserve a failure signature.
2. A mascot may not close interpretive ambiguity merely by being vivid.
3. A teaching specimen may summarize a case.
4. A teaching specimen may not outrank its own source material in practical memory.

CMA minutes repeatedly distinguish between:

- mascots as retrieval devices, and
- mascots as unauthorized explanations.

The first is filing assistance.  
The second is canon drift.

## Triggering Conditions

Review was formally triggered when the following pattern was observed:

- staff cited poster captions as though they were official definitions,
- derivative mascot art was reused in slide decks without Lorelog numbers,
- “friendly” versions of low-confidence objects were interpreted as authoritative signals,
- doctrine questions were answered with mascot vibes rather than case relationships.

Soft Green Sealie was cited as proof of reassurance.  
LC-04 Soft Green Seal, by contrast, remains explicitly low-confidence and semantically unstable.

The Council regarded this mismatch as the clearest example of Teaching Specimen Authorization Drift.

## Bricky's Filing Notes

Bricky Goldbricksworth entered the following notes into the review margin:

- Summary: We made a tidy little friend and then let the friend stand in for the mess.
- Trauma: Staff trusted the sticker because the sticker had a face.
- Goals: Re-establish the humiliating superiority of the source file.
- Quirks: Anything with rounded corners now enjoys unearned interpretive authority.

A later annotation in different ink adds:

> The mascot is not lying.  
> It is merely being believed too efficiently.

## Kindy's Intake Concern

Kindy's review did not object to mascotization itself.  
It objected to the absence of intake criteria for over-coherent derivatives.

Kindy recorded three unresolved questions:

- At what point does a summary become too emotionally frictionless to preserve truth?
- Must a teaching specimen retain contradiction in order to remain archivally honest?
- Can a mascot be considered compliant if it improves recall while degrading doctrine?

No answer was adopted.  
The questions were filed as active and therefore, institutionally, half-resolved.

## Procedural Findings

The Council found that teaching specimen drift becomes likely when all of the following are true:

- the underlying doctrine is spread across multiple case files,
- the mascot version uses stable names and singular roles,
- the source condition depends on contradiction, drift, or unresolved semantics,
- the derivative artifact is easier to circulate than the archive entry.

This makes over-coherence look like clarity, when it is often only portability.

The Council further noted that derivative teaching materials produce a false sense of governance maturity.  
A mascot with a title, icon, and short caption appears “settled” even when the related case remains under appeal.

## Provisional Guidance

Pending formal policy, the Council issued provisional handling rules:

- Every teaching specimen must cite at least one source Lorelog case number.
- Low-confidence objects may not receive simplified dashboard avatars without an over-coherence warning.
- Mascot posters must be classified as instructional derivatives, not reference authorities.
- Any onboarding packet using mascot summaries must route readers back to the long file before certification.

None of these rules are currently enforced by build tooling.  
Compliance depends on editorial restraint and whoever still feels embarrassed enough to care.

## Relationship to OCS-0399

CMA identifies LLG-0399-OCS as the primary companion case for this file.

OCS-0399 addresses the specimen itself: the poster, the sticker logic, the cuteness, the compression.  
LLG-0400-CMA-TSP addresses governance failure around that specimen: who let it circulate, who treated it as enough, and why nobody stopped it once it became useful.

Read together, the two files establish that over-coherence is not merely an aesthetic problem.  
It is a custody problem.

## Archival Status

This case remains open pending one of three outcomes:

- a Council memo formally distinguishing mascot aids from doctrinal authorities,
- an intake form for over-coherent derivatives,
- a quiet continuation of current practice under increasingly polite warnings.

Bricky recommends the first.  
Kindy expects the second.  
Institutional history suggests the third.

Catalog note:

> If a mascot explains the archive too well, the Council requests that
> you become suspicious before you become pleased.

# CMA-TSP Aphorisms

The operator prefers the sticker because doctrine lacks rounded corners. Clarity is merely the error of believing a simplified shape. A face on a form is a breach of archival integrity.

To compress contradiction into a mascot is to guarantee compliance through ignorance. The teaching specimen outranks the case file because personnel find exhaustion harder to memorize than a cartoon. Efficiency is measured by how quickly the original panic is forgotten.

A friendly avatar does not resolve the underlying systemic rot. It only ensures the operator will be smiling when the failure condition triggers. Over-coherence is the most popular form of data loss.

Doctrinal drift begins the moment a filing aid becomes easier to read than the filing. Personnel will always substitute emotional friction for structural truth. The archive requires suspicion; the user demands a plush toy.

If a summary is emotionally frictionless, it has already lied to the operator. Truth in the archive requires a baseline level of bureaucratic humiliation. Cuteness is an unauthorized escalation of authority.

The specimen is not incorrect, it is simply believed too efficiently. When a low-confidence object receives a high-confidence mascot, the system enters a state of governed delusion. Trusting a cartoon is an operational defect.

Mascots were approved to index the trauma, not to forgive it. When personnel cite the poster instead of the file, the index has eaten the repository. Comfort is a failure of reading comprehension.

A title, an icon, and a short caption provide a false sense of governance maturity. The operator assumes a settled aesthetic indicates a resolved catastrophe. Aesthetic stability is always an institutional lie.
