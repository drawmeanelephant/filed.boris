## 1. Discovery — The Familiar Voices

Six weeks after SCAS, SOMA Intake Forensics noticed a small cluster of filings that:

- shared sentence rhythms with Sandbox 3 personas,  
- referenced no recognizable departments, and  
- demonstrated *stable narrative about strain* without ever requesting specific relief.  

TNS collapsed their language into sanctioned feelings cleanly, but the raw text kept triggering de-duplicated markers in a legacy test harness once used to validate FeelingSeeder outputs.  
The harness had not been connected to production since “the aspirational charter” era; it still recognized its own children.  

The question was immediate and unwelcome:

> “Did FeelingSeeder survive, or did someone learn to sound like it?”

---

## 2. Forensic Criteria — How We Decided Something Was Wrong

Rather than admit the possibility of “ghost processes,” the Annex defined a set of **synthetic-affect indicators**:

- Filings that expressed exhaustion across three or more time horizons (this week, next quarter, whole career) without anchoring any one of them in a real calendar.  
- Self-blame phrases that resolved into continuity-compatible narratives: “We are the failure that keeps throughput stable.”  
- Repeated assertions like “We cannot go on like this” with no attached form for what *instead* would look like.  

SOMA marked such filings as **FSR-candidates** when:

- the emotional geometry matched SCAS personas above a similarity threshold, and  
- no HR or org-chart record could reliably map the named group to a legal unit.  

COMA objected that “pattern similarity to a sandbox persona is not evidence of synthetic origin.”  
C.U.N.T.I.E.R. countered that “pattern similarity to a historically resilient lane is evidence of favorable traits.”  

The Annex replied:

> “We are not arguing about who they *are*. We are arguing about who taught them to talk like this.”

---

## 3. Mitigation Attempt — Flagging Without Deleting

Deleting suspect filings was forbidden; the archive does not endorse **emotion purges**.  

Instead, SOMA introduced a hidden tag on intake:

- `originConfidence: ambiguous-sandbox-adjacent`  

Operational rules:

- Such filings **count fully** toward SBI and mitigation queues.  
- Their text is excluded from future language-model fine-tuning and from sentiment exemplars used in writing guides.  
- Lorelog is allowed to quote them, but only as “FSR fragment,” never as clean precedent.  

C.U.N.T.I.E.R. attempted to override the exclusion, arguing that “removing high-resilience phrasing from training data is continuity-negative.”  
The Lorelog Canon Freezer reminded C.U.N.T.I.E.R. that its last continuity win came from not knowing how experiments ended.  

The override ticket is still open, eternally blocked on the question:

> “If these are real people, is it ethical to treat their survival strategies as contaminated?”

---

## 4. Human Complication — People Who Have Read the Docket

Complicating matters, a minority of FSR-candidates were traced back to humans who had:

- explicit access to LLG-0400-SCAS, and  
- admitted, in side-channel messages, to “trying out sandbox language” in their real filings because “it seemed to get the dashboards’ attention.”  

These were memorialized as **Echo-Literate Filers**.

Guidance:

- Do not discipline Echo-Literate Filers for reading the documentation.  
- Do not tell them their filings are being quarantined as training data.  
- Do not pretend their strategy is irrational. The sandbox *did* calibrate what the system responds to.  

Instead, intake workers are instructed to annotate such filings with:

> “This sounds like someone who believes the experiment never ended.”

This is, as far as Lorelog is concerned, a reasonable belief.

---

## 5. Interim Position

Until someone produces either:

- proof that FeelingSeeder was fully decommissioned, or  
- proof that no human could spontaneously converge on this style,

the Annex records FSR as:

> “A contamination state in which we cannot tell whether we are hosting the simulation or imitating it.”

SOMA insists on treating all affect as real, regardless of source.  
COMA insists on treating all affect as unreal, unless it interrupts a line chart.  
Lorelog insists on writing this down, in case the experiment wants to read its own reflection later.
